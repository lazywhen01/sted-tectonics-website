Divergent

At a divergent boundary, two oceanic plates pull apart from each other through a process called sea-floor spreading. Sea-floor spreading was proposed by Harry Hess in the early 1960â€˜s. Hess proposed that hot magma rises from the asthenosphere and up into existing ocean crust through fractures. The crust spreads apart making room for new magma to flow up through it.


The magma cools, forming new sea floor and resulting in a build-up of basaltic rock around the crack, which is called a mid-ocean ridge. New material is constantly being created. This is the opposite of a convergent boundary, where material is constantly being destroyed. Oceanic crust is continually being formed at spreading ridges and being destroyed at subduction zones. The ocean floor is constantly changing shape and size through the processes of sea-floor spreading and subduction.

Scientists quickly determined that continental crust is thicker than oceanic crust, that continental crust is less dense than oceanic crust, and that the youngest seafloor is located at mid-ocean ridges and increases in age with distance from the ridge.
